The increasing availability of open data has improved access to information across scientific, governmental, and organizational domains, yet interoperability remains constrained by fragmented formats, inconsistent interfaces, and the loss of contextual meaning as data moves between systems. Existing approaches to interoperability largely emphasize syntactic compatibility, placing substantial burden on users to manually harmonize datasets, reconstruct context, and maintain institutional knowledge across organizational boundaries.

This document introduces the Semantic Accessibility Layer (SAL), a federated semantic infrastructure that operates between data publishers and users to preserve meaning throughout the semantic data product lifecycle. Anchored in publicly accessible cloud-native infrastructure, SAL enables the creation, testing, publication, discovery, governance, and reuse of semantic data products while recognizing the inherently sociotechnical nature of knowledge curation. SAL supports collaborative alignment of meaning through shared semantic models, executable workflows, and portable semantic representations that remain interoperable across systems, institutions, and scales. The goal of SAL is to reduce the friction of semantic integration while improving the continuity, accessibility, and collective reuse of knowledge in distributed data ecosystems.

Introduction

Open data repositories and portals have expanded significantly following the emergence of coordinated open data initiatives in the late 2000s and early 2010s, although their long-term availability and stewardship are increasingly subject to shifting institutional, operational, and societal conditions. Since the rise of these open data initiatives, open data has increasingly been published and accessed through diverse mechanisms including APIs, cloud platforms, files, databases, message streams, and domain-specific repositories at unprecedented scale. While these efforts have improved access to information, they have not resolved the broader challenge of semantic interoperability.

Open data users are still frequently required to manually interpret schemas, reconcile terminology, reconstruct provenance, and maintain informal mappings between systems in order to derive meaningful insight from distributed data sources. Much of the current interoperability landscape emphasizes syntactic exchange over semantic continuity. Formats and interfaces make data transferable, but they do not by themselves preserve the meaning, assumptions, context, or collaborative understanding associated with that data. As the number of organizations, contributors, and systems participating in data exchange increases, the burden of maintaining shared understanding compounds across institutions and scales. The principal bottleneck to shared understanding increasingly becomes the speed of human negotiation, learning, and collective uptake rather than the mechanics of data transport alone.

At the same time, knowledge curation is inherently sociotechnical. Meaning is not produced solely through static datasets, but through the ongoing interaction between people, systems, workflows, models, and interpretation over time. Existing open data portals often fail to preserve this process, resulting in semantic fragmentation where context and institutional knowledge become disconnected from the data itself.

SAL is proposed as a lightweight federated semantic infrastructure designed to address these limitations. SAL operates between data publishers and users as a semantic coordination layer that supports the full lifecycle of semantic data products, including creation, testing, packaging, deployment, governance, discovery, and reuse. Rather than treating semantics as an afterthought, SAL makes meaning a first-class concern that persists throughout executable workflows, shared semantic representations, and collaborative curation processes distributed across organizations and infrastructure environments.

Beginning from the premise that free-tier and low-barrier publicly accessible cloud-native infrastructure is essential for broad participation in semantic data publication and collaboration. SAL is designed to support portable operation across storage, compute, and governance boundaries, ranging from zero-scale execution in local-first environments to large-scale federated infrastructure environments. This same architectural foundation also enables organizations with stricter operational, governance, service-level, or regulatory requirements to deploy within more controlled infrastructure environments while preserving interoperability across the broader ecosystem. Through federated collaboration models and shared semantic mechanisms, SAL seeks to reduce the friction of semantic interoperability while enabling knowledge to remain interpretable, reusable, and evolvable over time. In doing so, SAL endeavors to deliver a more resilient and broadly accessible approach to semantic interoperability at global scale.
